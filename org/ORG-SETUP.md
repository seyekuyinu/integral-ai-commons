# Organization Setup

A guided process for defining your organization's human-centered AI operating model.

This is not a technical setup. It is a thinking process. The goal is to answer four questions clearly enough that every person in your organization — and every AI agent working alongside them — understands the ground rules.

Budget 2-3 hours for this. Do it with the people who will actually use AI, not just the people who manage them.

---

## Before you start

Gather the people who need to be in the room:

- Leadership or founders who set direction
- At least one person who works directly with customers or community
- At least one person who will be a daily AI user
- If your work affects a specific community, someone who represents that community

The answers to these questions should not come from one person. They should come from honest conversation.

---

## Question 1 — Who are we?

Write a plain-language description of your organization. Not your mission statement. Not your pitch. A description a stranger could read and understand.

Answer these:

- What do we actually do, day to day?
- Who do we serve, and what do they need from us?
- What do we believe about the people we serve?
- What makes our work ours — what would be lost if we were replaced by a generic version of ourselves?

**Write it down. Keep it under a paragraph.**

---

## Question 2 — What does good look like for us?

This is your organization's definition of flourishing. Not a growth target. Not a revenue number. What does it look like when your work is genuinely serving the people it's meant to serve?

Answer these:

- What does a good outcome look like for the people we serve?
- What does a good outcome look like for the people doing the work?
- What does a good outcome look like for the community our work touches?
- How do we know when we're achieving it?

**Write it down. Keep it concrete — describe what you would see, hear, or measure.**

---

## Question 3 — What never gets delegated to AI?

Every organization has decisions and relationships that belong to humans. Name yours explicitly. If you don't name them, they will quietly migrate to AI by default.

Work through these categories:

**Relationships**
Which relationships are ours to hold — not AI's to manage? Customer relationships? Community trust? Team dynamics?

**Decisions**
Which decisions require human judgment, full stop? Hiring? Pricing? Anything affecting someone's livelihood or dignity?

**Voice**
What must always sound like us? What communication, if it lost our voice, would lose its value entirely?

**Values**
What commitments do we hold that AI cannot be trusted to carry alone — because they require context, conscience, or accountability?

**Write a clear list. These are your non-delegables.**

---

## Question 4 — Who might be left out?

This is the question most organizations skip. Don't.

Answer these:

- Who benefits most from how we currently work?
- Who might benefit less — or be actively disadvantaged — as we bring AI into our work?
- Are there people our AI tools were not designed with in mind?
- How will we know if our AI use is creating new gaps rather than closing them?

**Write it down. Name specific groups, not abstract categories.**

---

## What to do with your answers

Once you have answered all four questions, you have three outputs:

**1. Your org identity statement**
One paragraph from Question 1. This goes into your `ORG-CLAUDE.md` so every agent session starts with a clear understanding of who you are.

**2. Your definition of flourishing**
The concrete description from Question 2. This becomes the standard against which you measure AI's impact on your organization.

**3. Your non-delegables list**
The list from Question 3. This goes into your `ORG-CLAUDE.md` as explicit instructions to every agent: these decisions and relationships stay with humans.

---

## A note on revisiting this

These answers will change. Your organization changes. The communities you serve change. AI changes.

Revisit this document every six months, or whenever something significant shifts — a new product, a new market, a leadership change, a failure that revealed a blind spot.

The operating model is not a policy document you file and forget. It is a living orientation that stays relevant because people keep returning to it.
