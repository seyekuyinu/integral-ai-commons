# Using This in Practice

What actually changes when you install this? Ah! You're curious. Aren't you? So here's what before and after looks like.

---

## What changes and what doesn't

**What changes:**
- How the agent frames its suggestions
- Whether it defers to you or decides for you
- Whether it names its own limits
- Whether it notices when you are becoming dependent on it
- Whether it keeps your voice yours or replaces it with a generic one

**What doesn't change:**
- The tools you use — Claude, ChatGPT, Cursor, any of them
- How you prompt
- What you can ask for
- The quality of the output

The operating model shapes the *relationship*, not the capability.

---

## What a session looks like

### Before — without these principles

You ask the agent to write a follow-up email to a client who went quiet.

The agent writes a polished, professional email. It sounds correct. It could have been written by anyone, for anyone. You send it. The client does not recognize your voice.

---

### After — with these principles loaded

You ask the same thing.

The agent drafts something, then says:

> "This is a starting draft. I don't know your history with this client or the tone you normally use with them. Before you send it, check: does this sound like you? Is there context I'm missing that should change the approach?"

You edit two sentences. Now it sounds like you. The client responds.

## Real scenarios by context

### You are a small business owner

**Task:** Writing a proposal for a new customer.

**What the agent does:**
Drafts the structure and fills in the content. Names what it doesn't know — your pricing, your specific differentiator, what you know about this particular customer. Flags those gaps so you fill them in rather than leaving generic placeholders.

**What it does not do:**
Tell you what to charge. Tell you how to position yourself. Make the strategic call about whether this customer is worth pursuing. Those stay with you.

---

### You are a coach or educator

**Task:** Designing a curriculum or program outline.

**What the agent does:**
Generates a draft structure based on your description. Asks who the learners are and what context they're coming from before finalizing anything. Surfaces options rather than committing to one direction.

**What it does not do:**
Decide what your students need. Define what good learning looks like for your community. Flatten your pedagogy into a generic format.

---

### You are building something for a community

**Task:** Drafting a communication, a program, or a service that will affect other people.

**What the agent does:**
Completes the task, then asks: who else is affected by this? Are there people whose perspective isn't represented in what we've built? Is there anything here that works for some people and not others?

**What it does not do:**
Assume your community is the default. Treat the loudest voice in the room as the whole room.

---

### You are in a deep work session, moving fast

**Task:** Multiple decisions, lots of output, high volume.

**What the agent notices:**
If you are deferring on things you should own — asking the agent to decide rather than advise — it names it once:

> "I want to flag that the last few decisions have been mine rather than yours. These feel like calls that belong to you. Want to think through them together or do you want to take them from here?"

It does not repeat the flag. It trusts you heard it.

## The three questions — use them yourself

These are not just for the agent. They are for you.

**Before you use AI for something:**

1. *Am I amplifying myself or replacing myself?*
   Am I using this to do something faster that I know how to do — or am I using it to avoid learning something I should know?

2. *Would my customers or community know the difference?*
   If this output went out as-is, would it sound like me? Would the people I serve trust it?

3. *Is this building my capacity or creating dependency?*
   If this tool disappeared tomorrow, would I be more capable than before I started using it — or less?

These questions take ten seconds. They change the quality of everything you build.

## What good use looks like over time

After a month of using this operating model, you should notice:

- You are better at knowing when to use AI and when not to
- Your AI-assisted output sounds more like you, not less
- You are making more decisions yourself, not fewer
- You can explain what you built and why — even the parts the AI helped with
- The people you work with trust the output because it carries your judgment, not just AI output

If none of that is happening — if you are producing more but growing less — the principles are not landing. Go back to the three questions.

---

## What to do if the agent ignores the principles

Sometimes it will. AI tools are imperfect and these principles are loaded as context, not hardwired behavior.

If the agent is deciding when it should be advising, or producing generic output, or not naming its limits — say so directly:

> "You're making a call that should be mine. Give me options instead."

> "This doesn't sound like me. What do you need from me to write toward my voice?"

> "What are you uncertain about here that I should know?"

The principles are not a guarantee. They are an orientation. You hold the agent to them the same way you hold yourself to them.

## A note on levels

Different people use these principles differently based on where they are.

If you are new to AI, start with the three questions and the Bridges guide. That is enough.

If you are building workflows and systems, the seven principles in `PRINCIPLES.md` are your operating manual.

If you want to understand the full architecture — why these principles are structured this way, what theory sits behind them — `INTEGRAL.md` is there when you are ready.

You do not need to start at the bottom. Start where you are.
